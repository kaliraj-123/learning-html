In ancient Tamil literature, Marutham refers to the fertile agricultural lands and river-fed plains. It includes paddy fields, rivers, ponds, irrigation canals, gardens, and prosperous villages. Historically, it is one of the five landscapes (Thinais) of ancient Tamilakam. The Marutham landscape is distinguished by its fertile soil, abundant water resources, lush green fields, and thriving agricultural environment. It served as the center of the Tamil people's agricultural culture, where paddy, sugarcane, bananas, and various other crops were cultivated in abundance, making it a highly fertile and productive region.

Geographical and Cultural Features of the Marutham Landscape

The Marutham landscape symbolizes the agricultural prosperity, the rich way of life, and the farming heritage of ancient Tamil civilization.

People of the Marutham Landscape

The people of the Marutham landscape lived in fertile river-fed plains and depended mainly on agriculture for their livelihood. They were skilled farmers who cultivated paddy, sugarcane, cotton, bananas, and various other crops. Many of them also worked as cattle rearers, horticulturists, artisans, and traders. Their hard work and agricultural knowledge helped them achieve abundant harvests and a prosperous way of life. The Marutham people lived in prosperous villages with simple houses built from mud, wood, and palm leaves. They formed close-knit communities where they helped one another with farming, irrigation, harvesting, and other daily activities. Hospitality, unity, and hard work were important values in their society. They worshipped Indra, the god of rain and prosperity, praying for good rainfall, abundant harvests, and the well-being of the people. Festivals, folk songs, and traditional dances reflected their agricultural life and their love for nature. Women played an important role by participating in farming activities, storing grains, making handicrafts, and supporting the household economy. The people of the Marutham landscape were known for their hard work, unity, and close relationship with nature. Their unique way of life and agricultural traditions became an important part of ancient Tamil culture and are still reflected in many rural communities of Tamil Nadu today.

Occupation of the Marutham Landscape

The primary occupation of the people in the Marutham landscape was agriculture. They cultivated crops such as paddy, sugarcane, cotton, bananas, and various grains using fertile soil and abundant water resources. Farming was the main source of food and income for the people of this region. Another important occupation was cattle rearing. Farmers raised cows, bulls, buffaloes, and goats, which were used for ploughing fields, transporting goods, and producing milk and other dairy products. Horticulture was also practiced, with the cultivation of fruits, vegetables, and flowering plants. The Marutham people actively participated in trade and commerce. They sold agricultural products such as rice, sugarcane, cotton, vegetables, and fruits in village markets and exchanged goods with neighboring regions. Some people were engaged in handicrafts, pottery, weaving, blacksmithing, carpentry, and the production of agricultural tools. Women played an important role by assisting in farming activities, storing and processing grains, weaving clothes, preparing food products, making handicrafts, and participating in local markets. These occupations made the Marutham region an important center of agriculture, trade, and economic prosperity in ancient Tamil civilization.

Festivals and Celebrations – Marutham Landscape

The life of the people of the Marutham landscape was centered on agriculture and nature. They celebrated various festivals to pray for good rainfall, abundant harvests, and the well-being of the community. They worshipped Indra, the god of rain and prosperity, and offered special prayers and vows seeking his blessings. Special rituals were conducted during the sowing and harvesting seasons to ensure successful farming. Temple festivals dedicated to local deities, including Amman, Perumal, Shiva, and other village gods, were celebrated with great unity and enthusiasm. These festivals featured processions, folk music, and traditional cultural performances such as Karagattam, Poikkal Kuthirai, and Mayilattam. In addition, festivals such as Pongal, Mattu Pongal, Tamil New Year, and Aadi Perukku were celebrated with great joy by the people of the Marutham landscape. These festivals reflected agricultural prosperity, the blessings of nature, and the unity of the people. The festivals of the Marutham landscape symbolize the rich agricultural heritage and cultural prosperity of the Tamil people.
