In ancient Tamil literature, Mullai refers to forests, grasslands, and pasture lands. It includes dense forests, shrublands, green meadows, small streams, and regions rich in natural resources. Historically, it is one of the five landscapes (Thinais) of ancient Tamilakam. The Mullai landscape is distinguished by its lush green forests, fertile grazing lands, diverse trees, vines, and wildlife. It was the homeland of the Ayar (pastoral communities) and is regarded as the symbol of cattle rearing and a way of life that existed in harmony with nature.

Geographical and Cultural Features of the Mullai Landscape:

The Mullai landscape represents forests, grasslands, and pasture lands in ancient Tamil culture. It is one of the five landscapes (Thinais) mentioned in Sangam literature. This landscape includes dense forests, green meadows, small streams, ponds, shrublands, and regions rich in natural resources. The climate of this region is generally moderate, with adequate rainfall and a cool natural environment. Mullai plants, bamboo, banyan trees, mahua trees, neem trees, and various forest trees are the major natural features of this landscape. The forests were rich in wildlife, birds, and other natural resources. The culture of the Mullai people was centered on cattle rearing. Grazing cows, buffaloes, goats, and sheep was their primary occupation. Dairy products such as milk, curd, butter, and ghee were their main source of livelihood. Collecting honey, gathering forest products, and small-scale trade were also important economic activities. Their settlements were located in peaceful surroundings, closely connected with nature. The people of the Mullai landscape worshipped Thirumal (Mayon) as their deity. They conducted rituals and festivals for the welfare of their cattle, good rainfall, and a peaceful life. Music, folk songs, flute music, and traditional dances formed an important part of their cultural life. Hospitality, patience, peace, and love for nature were highly valued in their society. The Mullai landscape symbolizes the harmony of the Tamil people with nature, their pastoral heritage, and their peaceful way of life.

people of the Mullai landscapes:

The people of the Mullai landscape lived in regions surrounded by forests, grasslands, and pasture lands, and depended mainly on cattle rearing for their livelihood. They were skilled pastoralists who grazed cows, buffaloes, goats, and sheep. Many of them produced and sold dairy products such as milk, curd, butter, and ghee. They were also engaged in collecting honey, medicinal herbs, and forest products. Their knowledge of nature and skills in cattle rearing helped them lead a prosperous life. The Mullai people lived in simple huts built with bamboo, wood, palm leaves, and other natural materials. They formed close-knit communities where they helped one another in grazing cattle, preparing dairy products, and carrying out daily activities. Hospitality, unity, and hard work were important values in their society. They worshipped Thirumal (Mayon) and prayed for the welfare of their cattle, good rainfall, and a peaceful life. Festivals, folk songs, flute music, and traditional dances reflected the beauty of nature and the lifestyle of the pastoral communities. Women played an important role by milking cows, preparing curd, butter, and ghee, making handicrafts, and supporting the household economy. The people of the Mullai landscape were known for their patience, close relationship with nature, and expertise in cattle rearing. Their unique way of life and pastoral traditions became an important part of ancient Tamil culture and are still reflected in many rural and forest communities of Tamil Nadu today.

Occupation of the Mullai Landscape:

The primary occupation of the people of the Mullai landscape was cattle rearing. They raised and grazed cows, buffaloes, goats, and sheep. They produced and sold dairy products such as milk, curd, butter, and ghee as their main source of livelihood. Honey collection, gathering medicinal herbs and forest products, and small-scale trade were also important occupations. They exchanged dairy products, honey, and forest resources with neighboring villages. In addition, cattle care, rope making, and crafting useful items from bamboo and wood also played an important role in their livelihood.

Festivals and Celebrations – Mullai Landscape

The life of the people of the Mullai landscape was centered on cattle rearing and nature. They worshipped Thirumal (Mayon) and conducted special prayers for the well-being of their cattle and for good rainfall. Village temple festivals, folk music, flute performances, and traditional dances were important features of these celebrations. Festivals such as Thai Pongal, Mattu Pongal, and Krishna Jayanthi were celebrated with great joy. These festivals reflect nature, cattle, and the rich cultural traditions of the Tamil people.
